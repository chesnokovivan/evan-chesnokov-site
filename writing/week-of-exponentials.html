The Week of Exponentials

Three things happened this past week:

  1. The US and Israel struck Iran and killed Ayatollah Khamenei — the supreme leader who had ruled the country with an iron fist for 35 years.
  2. Jack Dorsey, ex-founder of Twitter and founder of Block, laid off nearly half his company — four thousand people — explaining it with one line: "the tools have changed what it means to be a company."
  3. I was sick, drinking tea, and watching a system of Claude Code agents autonomously work through a task I'd described in a single prompt: each agent picked up where the previous one left off, tested, committed, passed it on. Without me. I was watching because, honestly, there was nothing left for me to do.

Three events from different worlds. But I have a feeling they share the same pattern.

We're used to thinking about the world as a normal distribution. Most events are average, predictable. Extremes are vanishingly rare. This works for height, weight, the size of apples on a tree. You'll never meet a person five times taller than average.

But some systems work differently — they follow power laws. And in those systems, extreme events aren't just possible. They're inevitable.

Here's how it works. Every year, thousands of lightning strikes hit forests. Most burn a single tree. Some burn a dozen. And once in a decade, one bolt starts a fire that scorches an area the size of a state. The cause is always the same — lightning. But the difference isn't in the cause at all. It's in the state of the forest. When it's too dense, when small fires have been suppressed for too long, the system enters a critical state. Any small disturbance triggers a cascade.

I think the world right now is that forest. And the cascades are happening simultaneously.

Let me start with the one closest to physics.

When Iran enriched uranium to 60%, most people (if anyone even noticed) read it as "just over halfway to a bomb." But uranium enrichment is a nonlinear process. 60% enrichment means 95% of the thermodynamic work is already done. The path from 60% to weapons-grade isn't forty percent of the effort — it's less than five. The window for a military response is weeks, not years. Our linear intuition breaks down precisely when we need it most.

Now AI. Late last year, I wrote about horses — about how the steam engine improved steadily for 200 years, but the replacement of 90% of horses happened in just twenty. METR, an organization that benchmarks AI agent capabilities, publishes a graph: the "time horizon" — the length of a task an agent can complete autonomously — doubles every 4–7 months. Recently the trend accelerated: a doubling every four months. Extrapolate the curve, and by 2027 agents will be autonomously completing month-long tasks. Progress is gradual. The moment of replacement is instant.

And then Dorsey. His letter is perhaps the first time the CEO of a ten-thousand-person public company says it plainly: the business is strong, profits are growing, but a smaller team with AI does more. Cutting half the workforce wasn't a response to a crisis — it was an acknowledgment of a new reality. The stock jumped 24% the same day. Yes, Jack admits he overhired during COVID, but I think his decision has less to do with that and more to do with the exponential growth in model capability and autonomy.

There's an analyst group that published a thought experiment this February — a fictional financial report written from the perspective of June 2028. The core question: what happens when the feedback loop has no brake? AI improves → companies cut headcount → laid-off workers spend less → companies invest more in AI → AI improves. They called it the "intelligence displacement spiral" and ended with the line: "The canary is still alive."

It's a scenario, of course, not a prediction. But when you read it the same week Dorsey lays off four thousand and METR updates their graph — the line between scenario and observation starts to blur.

Are you uneasy? I am. Not because of any single event, but because of the speed. Three systems — technological, geopolitical, economic — are simultaneously in a critical state. Each one is a dense forest where small fires have been suppressed for too long.

But what worries me most isn't the speed of change itself. It's what we're trying to make sense of it with. Most people have always thought on the surface: peasants didn't study monetary policy, factory workers didn't read Adam Smith. Communists didn't read much beyond Marx's religious texts. This isn't new. But the cost of shallow thinking used to be low: the world changed slowly, and those who didn't understand what was happening eventually caught up. Or ended up inside wars. In a world of power laws, that cost grows exponentially.

I'm not a misanthrope. It's not just about people. The tools we use to understand the world — reels, tweets, shorts, news feeds — are normal distribution machines. They output the average opinion, the trending reaction, the surface-level take. They are structurally incapable of helping you think about exponentials. We're trying to understand a power-law world with tools designed for a bell-curve one.

Nobody knows what to do about this gap. But I think the ability to stop and think — not react, but actually think — might be the only asset we have left. And it doesn't depreciate with the exponential.
